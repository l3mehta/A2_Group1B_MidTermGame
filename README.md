# A2_Group1B_MidTermGame

## Project Title

A Mind In Motion

—

## Group Number

Group 1B: Liza Mehta, Naveen Krishnabagavan, Marine-Chloe Vandame, Derica Li

## Description

First person driving game that simulates the lived experiences of the Bipolar Personality Disorder. The player makes a round trip driving from the Community Centre to the Recreation Centre and back.
The player begins the journey in euthymia, as they complete the round trip they must navigate the map while adjusting to unexpected state changes; a manic episode and a depressive episode.

—

## Setup and Interaction Instructions

Important keys:
W: Drive forward
S: Reverse
A: Steer left
D: Steer right
Space bar: Pause and start the game

Instructions:
Complete a round trip starting from the Community Centre to the Recreation Centre, finish the route by returning back to the Community Centre. Use the minimap and compass in the HUD for navigation help.

The game will pass through three states as the player drives; their occurrence cannot be controlled.
Euthymia: The map maintains its regular appearance and the mini map and compass work as normal. The car moves as expected.
Manic: The road signs, the mini map, and the compass may mislead the player. The car will feel much faster and difficult to control.
Depression: The car will feel heavy and slow, and the map will appear elongated and distorted.

Accessibility Options:
Reduced Effects Mode: Manic episode visual intensity will be reduced by 50%.
Skip Manic Episode: Plays through the route without the manic segment.

—

## Iteration Notes (Post-Playtest)

Reflection:
During playtesting, we noticed that players were overstimulated, confused about their orientation in relation to the destination, and weren’t sure whether to reference the mini map or the road. Once the manic state began, players grew frustrated trying to determine whether the road signs, mini map, and destination indicator were intentionally misleading or genuinely broken. Players were also confused by the visible state checkpoint and didn’t understand its purpose.

This feedback confirmed that our intentional distortion of the navigational affordances were landing, but the game play experience translated into player frustration rather than meaningful disorientation.
Three changes made based on playtesting:
Removed the state change visual checkpoint, now state changes begin and end unexpectedly without notifying the player
Expanded the map so the player has longer travel time, increasing the immersion of each state
Added a depression state where the player’s view distorts into a fish eye’s lens, the buildings appear elongated, and the sky darkens

—

## Iteration Notes (Post-Showcase)

Summary of key feedback:
Player controls and navigational mechanics not clear to players
Not enough distinction between states, especially depression and euthymia
Graphics and random sprites are overwhelming and motion sickness inducing
Implement standard first person controls instead of third person controls
Scale and proportion of graphics need to be improved for more realism
Camera clipping needs to be improved for more realism

Two planned improvements:
Restrict the mini map blackout to the depression state to improve ease of play during euthymia, and give the depression state a more distinct navigational identity
Randomize state duration and frequency using variables, instead of having each state trigger during fixed journey milestones, and lasting for half a level. Will make state changes more unpredictable and more accurately reflect the unexpected nature of episode occurrences in BPD.
